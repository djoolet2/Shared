## 12. Comprehensive Thermal & Cooling Analysis

### 12.1 Cooling Architectures & Design Philosophy

- **Legion (Slim 5 / 5 / Pro 5 / LOQ)**  
  - Use **Lenovo ColdFront** designs with dual‑fan, multi‑heatpipe layouts.  
  - Air intakes on bottom + keyboard zone; exhausts out rear + sides.  
  - Higher‑end Legions (5 16IRX9, Pro 5, Slim 5) use **phase‑change thermal material (e.g. PTM‑type pads)** on CPU/GPU in many configurations, which age better and can cut peak temps ≈ $5$–$10^\circ\text{C}$ vs cheap paste.  
  - LOQ models typically rely on **standard paste**, more prone to long‑term degradation.

- **Gigabyte Aero X16**  
  - Very thin chassis (~18 mm).  
  - Dual 12 V fans and dense fin stacks optimized for a slim profile.  
  - Cool operation for such a thin laptop, but at the cost of **higher fan noise**.

- **ASUS ROG Strix G16**  
  - Thicker, heavier chassis; dual‑chamber scheme.  
  - Larger raw volume but not dramatically cooler than well‑engineered Legions; ASUS often uses the extra thermal headroom to run parts faster rather than cooler.

**Takeaway:** Engineering, airflow paths, and TIM matter more than sheer thickness. Legions with ColdFront + phase‑change TIM are among the strongest designs here.

---

### 12.2 CPU Thermals Under Sustained Load

Typical sustained temperatures under heavy render (Cinebench‑class or long AE/Resolve exports):

- **Intel i7‑14650HX (Legion 5 16IRX9)**  
  - Sustained under heavy render: ≈ $85$–$95^\circ\text{C}$, with brief peaks to ~$100^\circ\text{C}$ in Turbo mode.  
  - Spec’d safe up to $100^\circ\text{C}$; when near that, you may see slight throttling and max fan noise.

- **Intel i7‑13650HX (LOQ 15IRX10)**  
  - Sustained: ≈ $88$–$96^\circ\text{C}$ under combined loads.  
  - Cooling is a bit weaker than higher‑end Legion 5; more likely to throttle if you run full CPU+GPU for long stretches.

- **Ryzen 9 8945HX (Legion Pro 5)**  
  - Sustained heavy render: ≈ $90$–$95^\circ\text{C}$; peaks slightly higher in synthetic extremes.  
  - Real‑world AE/Resolve: typically mid‑high $80^\circ\text{C}$.

- **Ryzen 9 8940HX (ROG Strix G16)**  
  - Gaming/editing: ≈ $85$–$92^\circ\text{C}$; uses larger cooler but similar end temps to Legion Pro.

- **Ryzen 7 HS series (8845HS / 7735HS / 7435HS – Slim 5, Aero, LOQ 15ARP9)**  
  - Editing workloads: ≈ $70$–$80^\circ\text{C}$ (much cooler than HX).  
  - Combined stress: can climb into low–mid $80^\circ\text{C}$ but generally more comfortable.

- **Ryzen AI 7 350 (Legion 5 OLED)**  
  - Editing workloads: often **below** $80^\circ\text{C}$; efficient architecture.  
  - Combined stress: mid–high $80^\circ\text{C}$.

**Implication:** HX CPUs (both Intel and AMD) **run hot by design**. That’s not failure, but you’ll want good airflow and perhaps undervolting if you push long renders. HS / AI parts run noticeably cooler and quieter.

---

### 12.3 GPU Thermals & Power

TDPs and typical sustained temps under GPU‑heavy workloads:

- **RTX 4070 8 GB (140 W – Legion 5 16IRX9, LOQ 15ARP9)**  
  - 4K gaming / heavy Resolve: ≈ $75$–$82^\circ\text{C}$.  
  - Combined CPU+GPU stress: $80$–$88^\circ\text{C}$.  
  - NVIDIA thermal soft limit around $87^\circ\text{C}$ – above this, clocks drop to hold that temp.

- **RTX 5060 8 GB GDDR7 (115 W – Legion 5 OLED, LOQ 15IRX10)**  
  - Resolve/4K workloads: ≈ $70$–$78^\circ\text{C}$.  
  - More efficient than 4070; GDDR7 reduces memory thermals and power.

- **RTX 5050 8 GB GDDR7 (115 W – Legion Pro 5, ROG Strix G16)**  
  - Combined load: typically $75$–$82^\circ\text{C}$.  
  - Slightly cooler than 4070 because of lower overall performance/power.

- **RTX 4060 8 GB (Legion Slim 5)**  
  - Typically **5–8°C cooler** than 4070 under same scenario.  
  - Good thermal headroom unless you use max turbo profiles.

- **RTX 4050 8 GB (Aero X16, ~95 W)**  
  - Editing: ~70–75°C; combined stress: ~80°C.  
  - Lower TDP = easier thermals.

**Thermal throttling:**  
On all modern RTX mobile parts, hard GPU thermal throttling usually starts at ≈ $87^\circ\text{C}$. Under real Resolve/AE loads, **none of these eight systems should regularly hit that** if fans are allowed to ramp and intakes are unobstructed.

- Editing: CPU ~75–85°C, GPU ~80–85°C.  
- Stress: CPU ~90–95°C, GPU ~82–88°C; can brush close to GPU soft limit.  
- Occasional reports of throttling in long heavy workloads; cooling tuned more for gaming bursts than workstation‑style sustained load.

**Lenovo Legion 5 15AKP10 (Ryzen AI 7 350 / RTX 5060 / OLED)**  
- OLED display itself adds a few W of heat vs IPS.  
- Editing: CPU typically < $80^\circ\text{C}$, GPU ~72–78°C.  
- Stress: CPU mid–high $80^\circ\text{C}$; among the cooler HX‑class‑like platforms.  
- Smaller screen area vs 16″ reduces GPU workload slightly; good overall thermals.

**Lenovo Legion Pro 5 16ADR10 (R9 8945HX / RTX 5050)**  
- Editing: CPU ~85–92°C, GPU ~75–80°C.  
- Stress: CPU ~95–98°C, GPU ~80–82°C.  
- Fans: ~58–59 dB at max.  
- CPU runs at the thermal edge in full AE renders; fine but benefits from undervolting and good airflow.

**Lenovo Legion 5 16IRX9 (i7‑14650HX / RTX 4070 / 32 GB)**  
- Editing: CPU ~80–88°C, GPU ~78–84°C.  
- Stress: CPU ~90–96°C, GPU ~85–88°C.  
- Fans: ~57–58 dB at max – mid pack.  
- With ColdFront and phase‑change TIM, plus moderate undervolt, you can cut ~5–10°C and a few dB; overall one of the best‑balanced thermal packages.

**Net:** for real Resolve/AE work (not torture tests) **none of these eight should hard‑throttle** if cooled reasonably. Long summer renders in hot rooms are the main edge case where LOQ 15IRX10 / 15ARP9 and ROG/Pro HX platforms can lose 5–10% sustained performance.

---

### 12.5 Stress Tests vs Real Editing

- **Prime95 / FurMark / combined torture**: 100% CPU+GPU load, pushing CPUs up to 20–30°C above idle; often unrealistic compared to editing.  
- **Cinebench R23 multi**: ~10–15°C hotter than actual AE renders.  
- **3DMark Time Spy**: bursts and varied patterns, unlike long 20–40 minute Resolve exports.  
- **Real editing**:  
  - Resolve timeline/exports use GPU at ~70–90% and CPU more moderately. Result: GPU temps ~3–5°C lower than pure synthetic stress.  
  - AE renders can be CPU‑heavier but still don’t fully match Prime95/Cinebench torture levels.

**Practical point:** If a laptop survives Prime95 + FurMark with only mild throttling, it will handle your editing workloads comfortably. For these eight, that is generally true, especially for the Legion 5 and Pro models.

---

### 12.6 Thermal Interface Materials & Long‑Term Behavior

- Standard **thermal paste** tends to dry and pump‑out over 12–24 months, often raising peak temps by $5$–$15^\circ\text{C}$ over time.  
- **Phase‑change pads** (like PTM‑type used in several Legion models) keep performance more stable over years and can reduce temps vs stock paste.  
- Likely beneficiaries: Legion Slim 5, Legion 5 16IRX9, Legion Pro 5 – they age better thermally than LOQ and some ASUS/Gigabyte units.

---

### 12.7 Cooling Pads, Undervolting & Fan Profiles

- External **cooling pads** can be **neutral or even negative** if they disrupt the internal airflow patterns; in some tests, pads increased GPU temps by a couple of degrees and reduced performance.  
- They can help in:
  - very hot rooms,  
  - poorly ventilated spaces,  
  - thinnest designs like Aero X16.  
- More reliable optimizations:
  - Mild CPU undervolt ($\approx 80$–$100\,\text{mV}$ where possible).  
  - Custom fan curves (Legion Vantage, Armoury Crate) tuned for your noise tolerance.  
  - Keeping vents unobstructed, using a stand.

## 13. Comprehensive Reliability & Quality Analysis

Thermals are only half the story. For a 3–4 year professional tool, you also care about:

- Display longevity and defects  
- System stability (random reboots, crashes)  
- Keyboard, hinge, and chassis integrity  
- RAM / SSD reliability  
- Battery behavior  
- Warranty/support quality  

### 13.1 Industry Context & Legal History

- Major brands (ASUS, MSI, Acer, Dell) have faced **class‑action suits and recalls** over:
  - Overheating and throttling far worse than advertised  
  - Battery defects (swelling, fire risk, sudden death)  
  - Hinge failures and screen damage  
  - Misleading upgrade claims  

- ASUS in particular settled a **multi‑million‑dollar class action** over ROG laptops with overheating, performance drop, and premature failure.  
- MSI faced legal action over unfulfilled GPU upgrade promises in GT72/GT80 series.  

This context matters: brand history signals how seriously they treat QC and support. Lenovo generally has a better reputation than ASUS and Acer in this regard.

---

### 13.2 Lenovo Legion & LOQ – Common Reliability Themes

**Positive:**

- Legion 5 / Pro lines generally have **solid build**, decent QC, and better‑than‑average support.  
- Reports of catastrophic failures (dead boards, hinges) are relatively rare compared to ASUS gaming lines.

**Issues to note:**

- **Sleep / power quirks:** especially on some Slim and LOQ units – failing to wake, random wakeups, fans spinning in sleep.  
- **LOQ 15IRX10:**  
  - Reports of random freezes or crashes in first 10–30 minutes post‑boot, sometimes persisting even after OS reinstall.  
  - Driver‑related BSODs (often GPU or power‑management related).  
- **LOQ 15ARP9:**  
  - Random reboots with generic Kernel Power 41 errors.  
  - Occasional display controller/TCON‑style failures (screen dead while system runs).  
  - More cases of stability issues after 12–24 months than Legion line.  

**Legion 5 & Pro 5 series:**  
- Overall fewer systemic defects reported.  
- Long‑term thermal paste degradation still applies where paste is used, but phase‑change TIM mitigates this.  
- Some minor reports of keyboard or touchpad looseness over time, but far less severe than ASUS hinge/keyboard issues.

---

### 13.3 ASUS ROG Strix G16 – Reliability Concerns

- Tied to ASUS’s broader history of:
  - Overheating and thermal design problems (previous ROG lines).  
  - Keyboard malfunction under high surface temperatures; keys become unresponsive during gaming/editing and only recover when cooled.  
  - Hinge cracking or loosening seen in other ASUS series.  

- Warranty practices: users report:
  - ASUS sometimes calling thermally‑induced keyboard failure “misuse”, denying coverage.  
  - Slower or less cooperative support vs Lenovo.

**Verdict:** even though **this exact G16 model isn’t in the lawsuit**, it inherits platform‑level risk. Combine that with only mid‑range GPU, 16 GB RAM, and high noise/heat, and it is a **hard sell versus Legion 5/Pro** for pro editing.

---

### 13.4 Gigabyte Aero X16 – Thin Design vs Reliability & Support

- Hardware itself is reasonably solid; biggest points:
  - Thin chassis = flex cable and hinge stress; sporadic reports of display flicker or blackouts when moving the lid (sign of cable/connection stress).  
  - High noise due to aggressive cooling.  
- Support:
  - Typically **mail‑in only**, few local centers, and long repair times (often 4–8 weeks).  
  - For a pro editor, that downtime is expensive.

**Verdict:** hardware is good; **support and downtime risk** are the main reliability negatives.

---

### 13.5 OLED Display Reliability (Legion 5 15AKP10)

Pros:

- Exceptional image quality for grading.  

Risks and aging:

- **Burn‑in:** static UI elements (NLE timeline, bars, menu UI) at high brightness over many hundreds of hours can leave ghost images.  
- **Luminance/color shift over time:** blue/green OLED subpixels lose brightness faster; after a few years of heavy use, peak brightness and color accuracy can drop by 10–20%.  
- For professional grading, this means the built‑in OLED may not remain trustworthy as your primary reference display long‑term.

Mitigation:

- Use dark themes, auto‑hide UI panels, moderate brightness.  
- Use external calibrated monitor for final grading if your livelihood depends on color accuracy.

---

### 13.6 RAM & SSD Reliability Patterns

- DDR5 is still relatively new; issues observed:
  - Some systems crash only under **very specific high‑load patterns** (heavy ML/upscaling) despite passing memtests – likely controller/compatibility rather than “bad stick”.  
  - LOQ 15IRX10 shows more anecdotes of RAM/power‑stability weirdness than Legion 5/Pro.

- SSDs:
  - Heavy edit workloads hammer SSDs with cache/preview files; after a few years, some consumer NVMe drives can:
    - Throttle under sustained writes (controller hits 70°C+),  
    - Or fail abruptly at high write counts.  
  - Also some reports of drives not waking properly from sleep/hibernate until reboot.

All eight use 1 TB NVMe; brand/model may differ by batch. For professional use, **have external backup and don’t rely on single‑drive safety** regardless of laptop.

---

### 13.7 Keyboard, Hinges, and Structural Issues

- **ASUS ROG Strix G16:**  
  - Heat‑induced keyboard issues (keys stop responding while the deck is very hot).  
  - Risk of hinge problems given ASUS track record.

- **Lenovo Legion (5/Pro/Slim):**  
  - Occasional reports of keyboard flex increasing over time; usually cosmetic/feel, not total failure.  
  - Hinges generally solid; far fewer horror stories than ASUS Zenbook/VivoBook lines.

- **LOQ series:**  
  - More plastic, more flex; not catastrophic but feels cheaper and may age worse structurally.  

- **Aero X16:**  
  - Thin design puts more stress on lid/hinge area; some users report intermittent display when moving the lid – often a hinge/cable problem.

---

### 13.8 Battery & Power System Issues

- Across the industry:  
  - Some laptops show **sudden battery death** (unit powers off immediately when unplugged).  
  - Rare but serious: battery swelling and chassis warping; mostly on problematic series (not especially common in these eight, but always a non‑zero risk with large Wh packs).

- For these models:  
  - Large batteries (60–90 Wh) and hot components mean **fast wear** if you frequently cycle them under load.  
  - Recommended for pro users: run plugged in with proper charging management; avoid repeated deep cycles during heavy workloads.

---

### 13.9 Software, Bloatware, and Stability

- ASUS Armoury Crate and some OEM tools are notorious for instability and high background load; removing them often improves reliability.  
- Lenovo Vantage is *relatively* better, but:
  - Can override Windows power plans,  
  - Sometimes causes unexpected throttling or fan behavior.  
- LOQ models in particular are sensitive to driver/BIOS versions; some crashes and BSODs have been fixed by updates, others not.

**Good practice:** after purchase, update BIOS and GPU drivers, then strip unnecessary OEM utilities while keeping those required for fan/power control.
